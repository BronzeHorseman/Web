About Us

Why 'Bronze Horseman', Anyway?

  • Because Peter the Great was an extraordinary man, and St. Petersburg is an extraordinary accomplishment. Creating a great city that ranked amongst the other capitals of the Enlightment era out of a malaria-ridden swamp was extraordinary, even if it did demand the lives of thousands of workers.
  • Because the statue itself is riddled with irony - erected by Catherine the Great as a way to honor her predecessor and elevate herself, it depicts a triumphant Peter riding a horse that is stomping on a snake - denoting Sweden, the country recently vanquished in the Great Northern War.
  • Because Pushkin's poem is an ironic and ultimately damning comment on tyranny - at once praising Peter the Great's leadership and the city that he erected, while damning both the city and society for catering primarily to the upper-class and nobility and their extravagant balls, the poem's protagonist is driven to madness and death. Cheery, eh?

In the end, the Bronze Horseman is a fitting symbol of both the glittering grandeur and the inherent contradictions of pre-World War I Russia - a nation poised for greatness that descended instead into barbarity.
